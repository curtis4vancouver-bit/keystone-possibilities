# Comprehensive Analysis of Canada Revenue Agency Determinations on Non-Eligible Dividends as Active Business Compensation in COVID-19 Benefit Clawbacks

## Introduction

The administration and subsequent auditing of pandemic-era income support programs in Canada—most notably the Canada Emergency Response Benefit (CERB) and the Canada Recovery Benefit (CRB)—have generated unprecedented volumes of administrative disputes, tax litigation, and public policy debate. As the immediate crisis of the COVID-19 pandemic receded, the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) shifted its operational posture from rapid, trust-based benefit disbursement to rigorous, retrospective compliance auditing. A central and highly contentious friction point in this post-payment verification phase has emerged between the CRA and self-employed owner-managers of Canadian-Controlled Private Corporations (CCPCs) regarding the exact characterization of non-eligible dividends.

The core of the dispute centers on a fundamental question of tax law and administrative policy: Do non-eligible dividends declared by a CCPC to its owner-manager represent passive investment returns, or do they constitute active business compensation that satisfies the statutory $5,000 minimum income threshold required to maintain eligibility for pandemic benefits? The CRA’s approach to this question has been inconsistent, leading to widespread confusion, punitive clawback demands, and a surge in applications for judicial review at the Federal Court of Canada.

This exhaustive research report provides an expert-level examination of the statutory mechanisms, internal CRA auditing guidelines, and the rapidly evolving jurisprudential landscape governing this issue. It details the critical February 2021 policy reversal regarding gross versus net self-employment income, dissects the corporate tax integration system that underpins the issuance of T5 slips for non-eligible dividends, and synthesizes recent rulings from both the Federal Court of Canada and the Tax Court of Canada. Furthermore, the analysis distills exact legal phrasing and procedural strategies derived from prevailing case law—such as the landmark decisions in Moncada v. Canada and Godin v. Canada—that can be strategically deployed by taxpayers and their counsel to compel the CRA to recognize a T5 slip as legitimate active business compensation, thereby ceasing unwarranted benefit clawbacks.

## 1. The Statutory and Policy [[ARCHITECTURE|Architecture]] of COVID-19 Income Supports

To fully grasp the nuances of the current clawback disputes, one must first understand the legislative intent and statutory construction of the pandemic benefit programs, which departed significantly from Canada's traditional social safety net infrastructure.

### 1.1 Legislative Intent and the Unprecedented Breadth of Eligibility

The Canada Emergency Response Benefit was enacted in March 2020 through the *Canada Emergency Response Benefit Act* to provide rapid, broad-based financial relief to workers who had lost income due to the pandemic. It provided a flat taxable benefit of $2,000 for a four-week period. The program was explicitly designed to bypass the traditional Employment Insurance (EI) system, which was structurally incapable of handling the sudden influx of millions of claims and, more importantly, systematically excluded vast segments of the modern workforce, including gig-economy workers, independent contractors, freelancers, and self-employed business owners.

The CERB established a relatively low baseline eligibility requirement: an applicant was required to reside in Canada, be at least 15 years old, and have earned a minimum of $5,000 in employment or self-employment income in 2019, or in the twelve months immediately preceding the date of their initial application. Additionally, applicants could not have quit their jobs voluntarily and, when submitting a claim, could not have earned more than $1,000 in employment or self-employment income for 14 or more consecutive days within the initial four-week benefit period, or for the entire duration of subsequent benefit periods.

When the CERB program sunsetted in the fall of 2020, it was replaced by the Canada Recovery Benefit (CRB) under the *Canada Recovery Benefits Act*. The CRB became available for two-week periods between September 27, 2020, and October 23, 2021, and maintained similar eligibility criteria, including the strict requirement that the individual must have earned at least $5,000 in employment or self-employment income in 2019, 2020, or during the 12 months preceding the application.

### 1.2 The Explicit Inclusion of Non-Eligible Dividends

Because the CERB and CRB were designed to capture individuals outside the traditional EI system, the definition of "self-employment income" was fundamentally broadened by the CRA and the Department of Finance. Small business owners and incorporated professionals rarely pay themselves through traditional T4 employment salaries; instead, they utilize the flexibility of corporate structures to remunerate themselves via dividends.

Recognizing this reality, the CRA explicitly published official policy guidance stating that non-eligible dividends count towards the $5,000 income requirement for both the CERB and the CRB. The CRA’s public-facing documentation, including its detailed Q&A web pages, confirmed that self-employed individuals could consider dividend income as qualifying self-employment income provided those dividends were "non-eligible dividends"—generally defined as those paid out of corporate income taxed at the small business rate. Furthermore, non-eligible dividends also counted toward the $1,000 income threshold that claimants were permitted to earn during a benefit period without disqualifying themselves from receiving the relief funds.

The explicit inclusion of non-eligible dividends was a vital policy choice, acknowledging that for a CCPC owner-manager, a non-eligible dividend is the functional and economic equivalent of a salary, representing the extraction of active business income generated by their own labor.

## 2. The Tax Mechanics of Corporate Integration and Dividends

To successfully argue before a CRA validation officer or a Federal Court judge that a T5 slip constitutes active business compensation, one must be able to articulate the technical mechanics of the Canadian corporate tax integration system. The CRA’s policy of accepting only non-eligible dividends, while excluding eligible dividends, is not arbitrary; it is deeply rooted in the *Income Tax Act* (ITA).

### 2.1 The Principle of Integration

The Canadian tax system operates on the principle of "integration." The theoretical goal of integration is to ensure that active business income earned through a corporation and subsequently distributed to an individual shareholder as a dividend is subject to roughly the same total amount of tax (combined corporate and personal tax) as if the individual had earned that income directly as an unincorporated sole proprietor.

Because corporations pay tax on their income before distributing the after-tax profits as dividends, the shareholder receives a dividend that has already borne a layer of taxation. To prevent double taxation, the personal tax system requires the shareholder to "gross up" the dividend to approximate the pre-tax corporate income, and then provides a non-refundable "dividend tax credit" (DTC) to account for the taxes already paid by the corporation.

### 2.2 Eligible vs. Non-Eligible Dividends: A Critical Distinction

The corporate tax rate a company pays depends heavily on its size, status, and the nature of its income, which in turn dictates the type of dividend it can issue. The distinction between eligible and non-eligible dividends forms the absolute crux of the CERB/CRB eligibility debate.

#### Eligible Dividends
Eligible dividends are generally paid by public corporations or non-CCPCs, or by CCPCs out of income that has been subject to the higher, [[general|general]] corporate tax rate. Because the corporation has paid a higher rate of tax, the shareholder receives a higher dividend tax credit. For the 2024 and 2025 tax years, eligible dividends are subject to a 38% gross-up rate and yield a 15.02% federal dividend tax credit. A CCPC tracks its capacity to pay eligible dividends through a notional account known as the General Rate Income Pool (GRIP). In the context of pandemic benefits, eligible dividends are generally viewed as passive returns on investment in large public equities (e.g., bank stocks, telecommunications companies), and therefore do not qualify as "self-employment income" for the purpose of reaching the $5,000 threshold.

#### Non-Eligible Dividends
Non-eligible dividends are paid from income that has been taxed at the much lower small business tax rate, thanks to the Small Business Deduction (SBD). For the 2024 and 2025 tax years, the gross-up rate for non-eligible dividends is 15%, and the corresponding federal dividend tax credit is 9.0301%. A CCPC tracks its non-eligible dividend capacity through the Low Rate Income Pool (LRIP).

The CRA correctly recognized that for the vast majority of small business owner-managers, non-eligible dividends represent the withdrawal of the active business income they personally generated. Consequently, the CRA’s policy equated the receipt of a T5 slip indicating non-eligible dividends (reported in Box 10 for the actual amount and Box 11 for the taxable amount) with active self-employment compensation, provided the individual was genuinely engaged in the operations of the business.

### 2.3 Reporting Mechanics: The T5 Statement of Investment Income

When a CCPC distributes a non-eligible dividend to an individual shareholder, it must issue a T5 slip, formally known as a *Statement of Investment Income*. The actual cash amount of the dividend paid is reported in Box 10, while the taxable amount (after the 15% gross-up) is reported in Box 11, and the corresponding dividend tax credit is reported in Box 12.

It is crucial to recognize that the title "Statement of Investment Income" is a systemic misnomer when applied to owner-managers. While the ITA classifies all dividends under the broad umbrella of property or investment income for general structural purposes, the specific CRA policy [[DIRECTIVES|directives]] regarding COVID-19 benefits pierce this corporate veil. For the purposes of CERB and CRB, the CRA treats the amount in Box 10 of the T5—when generated by a CCPC owned and operated by the claimant—as equivalent to the active self-employment income required to meet the $5,000 threshold.

## 3. The February 2021 Policy Reversal: Gross vs. Net Income

To successfully defend against a CRA clawback, it is vital to understand the administrative chaos that occurred in late 2020 and the resulting systemic policy correction in February 2021. This historical context provides a critical legal shield for taxpayers whose net income may have fallen below the $5,000 threshold due to business expense deductions.

### 3.1 The Genesis of the Clawback Crisis

When the CERB program was launched in the chaotic early weeks of the pandemic, the statutory language required applicants to have "self-employment income" of at least $5,000. Historically, under standard interpretations of the *Income Tax Act*, "self-employment income" strictly refers to net business income—meaning gross revenue minus all deductible business expenses.

However, the rapid rollout of the program led to significant miscommunications. The CRA subsequently admitted that its own call center [[AGENTS|agents]] had erroneously informed countless applicants during the program's initial weeks that eligibility was based on gross income. Acting on this government-provided information, hundreds of thousands of self-employed individuals, independent contractors, and gig workers applied for and received the CERB, knowing their gross revenues exceeded $5,000, even if their net income after expenses did not.

In late 2020, as the CRA transitioned to compliance verification, the agency began cross-referencing CERB applications with 2019 and 2020 tax returns. Applying the strict legal definition of net income, the CRA issued "educational" and enforcement letters to approximately 441,000 Canadians. These letters informed recipients that the CRA was unable to confirm they met the $5,000 net income requirement, thereby triggering a demand to repay the benefits received, which in many cases amounted to a staggering $14,000 debt.

### 3.2 The Ministerial Reversal and Its Retroactive Shield

The issuance of these clawback letters generated immense public backlash. Advocacy groups representing artists, actors, and self-employed professionals argued vehemently that it was unjust for the government to retroactively change the eligibility criteria after applicants had relied on explicit instructions from CRA [[AGENTS|agents]] to use their gross pre-tax income.

In response to mounting political pressure, the Government of Canada initiated a major policy reversal. On February 9, 2021, the Minister of Employment, Workforce Development and Disability Inclusion, alongside the Minister of National Revenue, announced a targeted amnesty. The new policy directive established that self-employed individuals who had applied for the CERB, and whose gross self-employment income met the $5,000 threshold, would not be required to repay the benefit, provided they met all other eligibility criteria.

This policy reversal carried profound retroactive implications and remains a vital defensive mechanism for taxpayers today:
- **Amnesty for Gross Income Claimants**: Individuals facing clawbacks based purely on the CRA’s retroactive net-income recalculation were absolved of the debt.
- **Reimbursement Mechanism**: The government recognized that some self-employed workers, intimidated by the initial letters, had already voluntarily repaid the CERB. Starting on June 15, 2021, the CRA began processing reimbursements for individuals who had repaid the benefit but actually qualified under the new gross income parameter.
- **Targeted Interest Relief**: To further ease the burden on those navigating COVID-related income support debt, the government announced that individuals who filed their 2020 income tax returns would not be required to pay interest on any outstanding income tax debt for the 2020 tax year until April 30, 2022.

### 3.3 Strategic Implication for Owner-Managers

For CCPC owner-managers defending the validity of their non-eligible dividends, the February 2021 policy reversal is a critical tool. If a CRA auditor attempts to disqualify a taxpayer by aggressively discounting corporate or personal business expenses in an attempt to push the net economic benefit below $5,000, the taxpayer can invoke the February 9, 2021 directive. This forces the CRA to assess eligibility based on the gross receipts of the business prior to the declaration of dividends and the deduction of operational expenses.

## 4. CRA Internal Audit Procedures and the Evidentiary Burden

When a taxpayer's CERB or CRB file is flagged for a validation review, the assessment is governed by internal CRA protocols, which are significantly informed by the principles contained within the CRA's *Income Tax Audit Manual* (ITAM). Understanding how validation officers approach these files is essential for mounting a successful defense.

### 4.1 The Role of ITAM Chapter 24 and Chapter 27

While CERB and CRB validation reviews are ostensibly administrative processes, the reviewing officers utilize auditing heuristics drawn directly from the ITAM. Chapter 24 of the ITAM focuses specifically on audits of transactions involving related persons, corporations, and shareholders, with a heavy emphasis on dividends and the potential for sham transactions. Chapter 27 details procedures for auditing various forms of self-employment and business incomes.

The primary objective of the CRA during a pandemic benefit validation review is to differentiate between genuine active business remuneration and aggressive, retroactive tax-planning shams. Because the eligibility threshold of $5,000 is relatively low, the CRA is actively aware of the potential for an owner-manager to retroactively declare a $5,000 non-eligible dividend at the end of the fiscal year purely to secure $14,000 in CERB or $26,000 in CRB payments, despite the business being essentially dormant.

If an owner-manager claims CERB eligibility based on a T5 showing non-eligible dividends, the validation officer will heavily scrutinize the file to ensure the dividends were not artificially declared. The auditor will look for economic substance behind the paper trail.

### 4.2 The Burden of Contemporaneous Documentary Evidence

The evidentiary burden to prove eligibility rests squarely on the taxpayer. As explicitly noted in CRA guidelines and affirmed by subsequent court decisions, a T5 slip alone, generated months after the benefit periods, is often insufficient to satisfy a skeptical auditor. The CRA requires robust, contemporaneous documentary evidence that the dividend was compensation for actual work performed during the qualifying period (2019, 2020, or the prior 12 months).

Acceptable evidence to substantiate a claim of active self-employment income includes a combination of the following:
- **Detailed Invoices**: Invoices for services rendered must include the date of the service, a description of the work, the client’s name, and the applicant's or company's name.
- **Financial Documentation**: Bank statements, statements of account, or bills of sale showing the receipt of payment for the services provided.
- **Business Operations Proof**: Documentation showing income was earned from carrying on a "trade or business," including business licenses, contracts, proof of advertising, and comprehensive lists of expenses to support the net result of earnings.

When an individual pays themselves via non-eligible dividends, the CRA validation officer expects to see a clear economic pipeline: evidence of work performed by the owner-manager → client invoices → gross revenue entering the corporate bank account → a corresponding non-eligible dividend declared and transferred to the owner-manager’s personal bank account. A break in this chain, or a failure to provide the underlying documentation supporting the corporate revenue, is the leading cause of benefit denials.

## 5. Jurisprudential Review: The Tax Court of Canada (TCC)

To build an unassailable legal argument regarding the validity of non-eligible dividends, it is crucial to understand the distinct roles of Canada's federal judicial bodies. The Tax Court of Canada (TCC) holds exclusive original jurisdiction to hear and determine appeals on matters arising under the *Income Tax Act*. While the TCC does not directly adjudicate CERB or CRB eligibility (which falls to the Federal Court), TCC jurisprudence defines the legal reality of the T5 slip and the obligations of both the taxpayer and the CRA regarding dividend declarations.

### 5.1 The Legally Binding Nature of a T5 Slip

A recurring theme in TCC jurisprudence is that taxpayers cannot retroactively rewrite their corporate history to suit their immediate tax or benefit needs, and conversely, the CRA cannot arbitrarily ignore validly issued tax documentation when it is convenient to do so.

In the highly relevant case of *Kufsky v. The Queen* (2019 TCC 254, later affirmed by the Federal Court of Appeal in 2022 FCA 66), the taxpayer was the sole shareholder of a CCPC that provided decorating services. The corporation had material unpaid tax debts. The corporation's accountants filed adjustment requests reporting dividend income paid to the taxpayer and issued corresponding T5 slips for the years 2009, 2010, and 2011. The CRA subsequently invoked Section 160 of the *Income Tax Act*—a powerful collection tool allowing the CRA to chase assets transferred from a tax debtor to a non-arm's-length party—and assessed the taxpayer personally for the corporation's debt, up to the value of the dividends received.

The taxpayer argued that she did not recall authorizing the dividends and pointed out that there were no formal corporate director resolutions in the minute book documenting their payment, implying the T5 slips were essentially bookkeeping fictions. The Tax Court, and subsequently the Federal Court of Appeal, firmly rejected this argument. The courts ruled that absent clear language voiding a transaction, taxpayers who accept a payment of dividends for tax purposes—and benefit therefrom—must live with the resulting legal and tax liability consequences, regardless of technical deficiencies under corporate law.

A similar application of Section 160 occurred in the recent decision *McCague v The King* (2025 TCC 59), where the Tax Court confirmed that even 50-50 shareholders who receive dividends from a tax-debtor corporation can be held derivatively liable, demonstrating the CRA's aggressive pursuit of dividend distributions.

#### Strategic Implication for CERB/CRB Clawbacks
The *Kufsky* and *McCague* precedents represent a powerful weapon for taxpayers fighting benefit clawbacks. The CRA routinely relies on T5 slips as absolute, legally binding evidence of an economic transfer when applying Section 160 to collect corporate tax debts. Therefore, a taxpayer facing a CERB clawback can forcefully argue the doctrine of legal estoppel: The CRA cannot logically or legally assert that a T5 slip is a valid, binding realization of income when assessing derivative tax liabilities, while simultaneously claiming that the exact same T5 slip is "insufficient evidence" of income when the taxpayer attempts to use it to prove the $5,000 threshold for pandemic benefits.

### 5.2 The Danger of Misinterpreting Corporate Law (The McClurg Precedent)

While the T5 is a legally binding document, taxpayers must be exceedingly careful about how they characterize the nature of their dividends in written submissions.

In the seminal 1990 Supreme Court of Canada decision *McClurg v. Canada* ([1990] 3 S.C.R. 1020), the Court examined the nature of discretionary dividends declared to shareholders in a closely held company. The Court noted that dividends generally constitute a return on an investment based on the rights attached to a class of shares, rather than a direct return for work or service provided to the company.

As will be seen in the Federal Court analysis below, taxpayers who attempt to quote *McClurg* to CRA validation officers in an effort to legitimize their non-eligible dividends inevitably sabotage their own cases. By arguing that the dividend was purely a return on passive investment, the taxpayer effectively admits that the income was not "self-employment income" generated by active labor, which is the foundational requirement of the CERB and CRB legislation.

## 6. Jurisprudential Review: The Federal Court of Canada (FC)

When a taxpayer exhausts the CRA's internal validation and second-level administrative review processes resulting in a final denial, their only recourse is to file a *Notice of Application for Judicial Review* at the Federal Court of Canada within 30 days.

It is vital to understand the Federal Court’s jurisdiction in these matters. The Federal Court does not conduct a *trial de novo*; it does not hear new evidence, nor does it decide whether the taxpayer was actually eligible for the benefits. Instead, the Court evaluates the CRA officer's administrative decision against the standard of "reasonableness," as established by the Supreme Court of Canada in *Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration) v. Vavilov* (2019 SCC 65).

A decision is deemed reasonable if it is based on an "internally coherent and rational chain of analysis" and is "justified in relation to the facts and law that constrain the decision maker". If the CRA officer ignored evidence, failed to provide adequate reasons, or acted unfairly, the Federal Court will quash the decision and remit the matter back to the CRA for redetermination by a different officer.

An analysis of recent Federal Court decisions provides a precise roadmap of what constitutes a "reasonable" versus "unreasonable" denial when non-eligible dividends are involved.

### 6.1 Unsuccessful Strategies: The Fatal Error of Retroactive Documentation

Taxpayers who engage in post-facto, retroactive tax planning to secure CERB/CRB funds face near-certain defeat in Federal Court. A highly illustrative 2023 Federal Court decision, summarized by tax expert Jamie Golombek, highlights the pitfalls of aggressive non-eligible dividend claims.

- **The Facts**: A Quebec taxpayer applied for and received CERB and CRB for numerous periods between March 2020 and October 2021. During a CRA review in early 2022, the taxpayer claimed he met the income threshold by paying himself dividends as a self-employed owner-manager. He produced a T5 slip for the 2020 taxation year showing dividend income of $7,479.60.
- **The CRA's Findings**: The CRA agent investigated the corporate history and noted that the taxpayer had not reported any income or salary since 2013, and the corporation had paid zero dividends in the previous nine years. The taxpayer was unable to provide details of the work he carried out, when it was performed, and could not produce a single invoice or receipt. Crucially, the sequence of transactions between the corporate and personal bank accounts was irregular, and the T5 slip for $7,479.60 was only completed retroactively in September 2023—long after the CRA had initiated its review and requested proof of income.
- **The Legal Argument and Ruling**: In court, the taxpayer fatally attempted to rely on the Supreme Court's *McClurg* decision, arguing that his dividends "constitute a return on an investment and not a return for work or a service". The Federal Court judge firmly rejected the application, noting that "without proof that the [taxpayer] performed work and was paid, it was not unreasonable for the officer to conclude that he did not meet the eligibility criteria". The retroactive nature of the September 2023 T5 slip rendered the evidence "not sufficiently credible," and the CRA's denial was deemed entirely reasonable and justified.

A similar outcome occurred in *Abouamin v. Canada* (2023), where the applicant sought judicial review of a CERB and CRB denial based on the $5,000 self-employment income threshold. The Court dismissed the application, affirming that given the billions of dollars disbursed, the CRA is legally justified in refusing to take recipients' words at face value and insisting on acceptable documentary proof.

### 6.2 Successful Strategies: Procedural Fairness and Ignored Evidence

Conversely, when a taxpayer provides contemporaneous documentary evidence that a CRA officer subsequently ignores or dismisses without logical justification, the Federal Court routinely strikes down the decision as unreasonable, demonstrating a robust commitment to procedural fairness.

#### The Moncada Precedent (Ignored Invoices)
In *Moncada v. Canada (Attorney General)* (2023 FC 114), the applicant successfully obtained CRB for several periods in late 2020. During a subsequent validation process, the applicant submitted three sets of documents, critically including three distinct invoices for general trade and renovation work for three different clients. The invoices totaled $5,380—perfectly matching the stated income in his 2019 tax return—and detailed the applicant's name, the type of service, the client, and the amount charged.

Despite this, the CRA validation officer denied the application, boilerplateing the decision by stating there was "insufficient documentation to support income." The applicant sought judicial review. The Federal Court ruled resoundingly in the taxpayer's favor, declaring the CRA's decision unreasonable. The Court emphasized that the CRA officer had completely failed to mention the three invoices in their reasons and, more importantly, failed to explain why they were inadequate as proof of meeting the income eligibility requirement. Citing *Crook v Canada* and *Sjogren v Canada*, the Court affirmed that a lack of justification for rejecting a form of proof explicitly contemplated by the CRB guidelines constitutes an unreasonable administrative decision. The matter was remitted back for redetermination by a different officer.

#### The Godin Precedent (Context and Procedural Fairness)
In *Godin v. Canada (Attorney General)* (2024 FC 1386), the CRA reviewed the eligibility of a taxpayer who had received the CRB, CRCB, CRSB, and CERB. During the second-level review, the CRA concluded he did not meet the $5,000 threshold and had not suffered a sufficient reduction in income due to COVID-19. Mr. Godin argued the decision was unfair, as he had provided documentation showing income above $5,000 and explained that a devastating house fire in 2019 had destroyed his tools, artificially suppressing his baseline income just before the pandemic struck. The Federal Court intervened, focusing strictly on procedural fairness. The Court ruled that government decision-makers have a legal duty to provide individuals a fair chance to understand the case against them and must adequately consider exculpatory contexts (such as the house fire) before issuing a final, punitive decision. The process was deemed unfair, and reconsideration was ordered.

#### The Doheney Precedent (Incompetent Counsel)
In *Doheney v. Canada (Attorney General)* (2025 FC 1532), the applicant, a wellness consultant and yoga instructor, was found ineligible for the CRB by a second-level CRA officer because she failed to provide proof regarding her business-related expenses for the 2019 tax year. The applicant applied for judicial review, presenting evidence that she had paid $10,000 to lawyers who had actively—and incorrectly—advised her not to comply with the CRA's lawful request for proof of 2019 expenditures. Recognizing "exceptional circumstances constituting incompetent legal representation," the Federal Court exercised its discretion to set aside the CRA's decision, granting the taxpayer a crucial second opportunity to present her documentation free from the taint of bad legal advice.

## 7. Strategic Blueprint: Forcing CRA Recognition and Ceasing Clawbacks

To successfully force the CRA to recognize a T5 as active business compensation and cease a CERB/CRB clawback, a practitioner or self-represented taxpayer must execute a multi-pronged strategy. This strategy must combine the February 2021 gross income policy, the CRA's own published guidelines on non-eligible dividends, the binding nature of tax documentation under *Kufsky*, and the administrative law standards articulated in *Moncada* and *Vavilov*.

### 7.1 Compiling the Evidentiary Package (First and Second-Level Reviews)

During the initial 45-day validation request, or within the 30-day window to request a Second Administrative Review, it is wholly insufficient to simply submit a T5 slip. The submission must proactively address the CRA auditor's internal checklist regarding the authenticity of the active business income.

The submission package must comprehensively include:
1. **The T5 Slip & Tax Returns**: Explicitly pointing to Box 10 (Actual amount of dividends other than eligible dividends) and matching this to the personal tax return.
2. **Contemporaneous Client Invoices**: Demonstrating the nature of the work, dates, and amounts billed.
3. **Corporate Bank Statements**: Showing the gross revenue entering the CCPC resulting from the owner-manager's direct labor.
4. **Proof of Dividend Transfer**: Bank records showing the transfer of funds from the corporate account to the personal account, matching the T5 declaration.

### 7.2 Exact Legal Phrasing for CRA Submissions and Judicial Review

The following legal phrasing should be directly incorporated into written responses to CRA Validation Officers during the administrative review stages, or utilized in the sworn affidavits and memorandums of fact and law supporting a *Notice of Application for Judicial Review* at the Federal Court. This language legally corners the CRA by relying on their own statutory interpretations and binding jurisprudence.

#### Argument 1: CRA's Explicit Recognition of Non-Eligible Dividends (The Integration Argument)
> *"The Applicant respectfully submits that the Canada Revenue Agency’s published policy [[DIRECTIVES|directives]] explicitly acknowledge that 'non-eligible dividends' constitute qualifying self-employment income for the purposes of the $5,000 eligibility threshold for the Canada Emergency Response Benefit (CERB) and the Canada Recovery Benefit (CRB). The Applicant is the owner-manager of a Canadian-Controlled Private Corporation (CCPC). Under the fundamental principles of Canadian corporate tax integration, the Applicant possesses the legal discretion to remunerate their active labor via non-eligible dividends distributed from the Low Rate Income Pool (LRIP), reflecting active business income taxed at the small business rate. The issuance of the T5 slip herein is not indicative of a passive investment return, but rather the economic realization of active business compensation generated by the Applicant's personal labor. Denying eligibility on the basis that the income was distributed as a non-eligible dividend rather than a T4 salary constitutes a direct contravention of the CRA’s stated policy parameters regarding COVID-19 benefit eligibility."*

#### Argument 2: Invoking the February 2021 Gross Income Shield
> *"Should the Minister attempt to disqualify the Applicant by recalculating net self-employment income to a figure below $5,000 through the aggressive deduction of business expenses, the Applicant invokes the Government of Canada’s explicit policy directive issued by the Minister of Employment on February 9, 2021. This directive unequivocally established that self-employed individuals who applied for the CERB, and whose gross self-employment income met the $5,000 threshold, are not required to repay the benefit, provided all other eligibility criteria are met. The Applicant’s contemporaneous invoices and corporate bank deposits, enclosed herein, irrefutably demonstrate gross receipts resulting from the Applicant's labor exceeding the statutory minimum."*

#### Argument 3: The Binding Nature of Tax Documentation (The Kufsky Estoppel)
> *"The Applicant submits that the T5 slip provided is a binding legal document representing an economic transfer. As established by the Tax Court of Canada in Kufsky v. The Queen (2019 TCC 254, aff'd 2022 FCA 66) and recently affirmed in McCague v The King (2025 TCC 59), the CRA routinely relies upon the issuance of dividend slips as absolute proof of an economic transfer when assessing derivative tax liabilities under Section 160 of the Income Tax Act. It represents a fundamental breach of administrative consistency and legal estoppel for the CRA to treat a T5 slip as binding proof of income when assessing tax liabilities, while simultaneously dismissing that exact same documentation as 'insufficient evidence' of income when a taxpayer relies upon it to establish eligibility for pandemic relief benefits."*

#### Argument 4: Preempting Unreasonable Denials (The Moncada Standard)
> *"The Applicant has provided a comprehensive evidentiary record, including the official T5 slip, corresponding corporate bank statements, and contemporaneous client invoices, unequivocally demonstrating the active generation of the non-eligible dividend income. Pursuant to the Federal Court’s binding precedent in Moncada v. Canada (Attorney General) (2023 FC 114), any administrative decision that fails to actively consider and provide an internally coherent explanation for rejecting these specific documentary proofs renders the decision unreasonable. Furthermore, under the administrative law framework established by the Supreme Court of Canada in Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration) v. Vavilov (2019 SCC 65), a failure by the reviewing officer to meaningfully grapple with the contemporaneous evidence of active labor provided herein will constitute grounds for judicial review."*

### 7.3 Proceeding to the Federal Court

If the CRA issues a final second-level denial, the taxpayer must file a *Notice of Application* with the Federal Court within 30 days of receiving the decision.

The application must be framed strictly as a judicial review of administrative action, not an appeal of a tax assessment. The legal argument must not ask the Federal Court to declare the taxpayer eligible; rather, it must ask the Court to find that the CRA's second reviewer acted "unreasonably" under the *Vavilov* standard by ignoring the contemporaneous evidence connecting the T5 non-eligible dividends to the gross active business income generated by the owner-manager. If the application successfully demonstrates that the CRA officer ignored invoices or failed to apply the February 2021 gross income policy, the Federal Court will quash the CRA's demand for repayment and order the matter to be reviewed afresh by a different, unbiased officer, binding them to properly weigh the submitted evidence.

## Conclusion

The Canada Revenue Agency's ongoing campaign to audit and claw back CERB and CRB payments from self-employed owner-managers has exposed a fundamental tension between the complex realities of Canadian corporate tax integration and the blunt administrative mechanisms of emergency relief programs. While the CRA has explicitly stated in its published guidelines that non-eligible dividends count toward the $5,000 income requirement, validation officers frequently default to a posture of deep skepticism, treating T5 slips as suspect indicators of passive investment rather than legitimate active remuneration.

The jurisprudential record clearly indicates that taxpayers cannot rely solely on post-facto tax filings or retroactively generated T5 slips. The Federal Court heavily penalizes a lack of contemporaneous documentation, viewing retroactive tax planning as a circumvention of the legislative intent of the pandemic programs. However, by strategically weaponizing the February 2021 policy reversal regarding gross income, and by assembling a robust, unassailable evidentiary package linking client invoices to corporate deposits and subsequent non-eligible dividend declarations, an owner-manager can effectively force the CRA's hand.

By employing precise legal phrasing derived from the *Vavilov*, *Moncada*, and *Kufsky* precedents, taxpayers and their counsel can successfully argue that the CRA is legally bound to recognize their non-eligible dividends as active business compensation. If the CRA fails to provide a rational, internally coherent explanation for dismissing this evidence, the Federal Court has demonstrated a clear and consistent willingness to intervene, quash the unreasonable administrative decision, and halt the unjust clawback of pandemic benefits.
