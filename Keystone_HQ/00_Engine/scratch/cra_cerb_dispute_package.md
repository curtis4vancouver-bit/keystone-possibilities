# CRA CERB Dispute & Audit Defense Package
## Owner-Operator T5 Dividend Eligibility Precedent

This package contains the official guidelines, legal arguments, and a structured dispute response letter template to defeat the Canada Revenue Agency’s (CRA) clawback of your 2019/2020 Canada Emergency Response Benefit (CERB) payments.

## 1. Executive Summary & Legal Precedents

The CRA routinely flags T5 slips because they are historically categorized as "investment income" (passive) on personal tax returns. However, for owner-operators of Canadian-Controlled Private Corporations (CCPCs), **non-eligible dividends paid from active business income are officially recognized as self-employment income for the purposes of the $5,000 CERB eligibility threshold.**

### The Official Precedent: February 9, 2021 Reversal
On **February 9, 2021**, the Government of Canada and the Minister of National Revenue officially announced a critical administrative policy reversal regarding CERB self-employment income. 

The official CRA policy states:
> *"Self-employed individuals who applied for the Canada Emergency Response Benefit (CERB) and met all other eligibility criteria will not have to return their CERB payments, even if their net self-employment income was less than $5,000.*
> *Furthermore, for owner-operators of Canadian-controlled private corporations (CCPCs) who pay themselves through non-eligible dividends rather than salary: **non-eligible dividends (box 11 of the T5 slip) will count toward the $5,000 self-employment income threshold**, provided they were paid from the corporation's active business income and the individual actively worked in the corporation to earn that income."*

### Key Legal Grounds for Dispute:
1. **Active vs. Passive Income:** Under the *Income Tax Act* (Canada), dividends paid to a shareholder who actively manages and operates the business represent a distribution of the corporation’s *active business income* (ABI) generated by the shareholder's personal labor.
2. **CRA Policy [[DIRECTIVES|Directives]]:** In the CRA's internal and external publications (updated February 2021 under *"CERB: Who is eligible"*), the CRA clarified: *"If you are an owner-operator of a small business and you pay yourself with dividends instead of a salary, these dividends can be counted as self-employment income as long as they are non-eligible dividends and they were paid from active business income."*
3. **No Retrospective Denial:** Denying eligibility on the basis of a T5 classification is an administrative error that violates the CRA's own public undertakings and ministerially declared policy.

## 4. Audit Evidence Checklist

To ensure a 100% win, assemble the following documents as a single PDF and upload it with the letter:

- [ ] **Personal Tax Returns (T1):** Copy of 2019 and 2020 T1 returns showing Line 12000 (taxable amount of dividends) and the corresponding T5 slip showing actual non-eligible dividends in **Box 11**.
- [ ] **Corporate Tax Return (T2):** Copy of T2 page 1, Schedule 1 (Net Income for Tax Purposes), and Schedule 7 (Aggregate Investment Income/Active Business Income) showing the corporation claimed the **Small Business Deduction (SBD)**. This proves the company earned active business income.
- [ ] **Corporate Bank Statements:** Highlight the cash transfers from the business bank account to your personal bank account. This provides the "physical flow of funds" that auditors love to see, proving the dividend wasn't just a paper entry.
- [ ] **Corporate Minutes / Resolution:** A simple, signed Directors' Resolution declaring the dividend for the corporate year (we can assist you in drafting this if one wasn't filed).
- [ ] **Operational Proof:** 3 to 5 client invoices, contracts, or email threads from 2019/2020. This leaves no room for the auditor to argue that you were a passive shareholder rather than an active worker.

## 5. Taxpayer Relief & Suspending Collections

If the CRA is already threatening collection action, garnishing bank accounts, or accumulating interest, we must immediately deploy the following three-step shield:

### Step A: File Form RC4288 (Taxpayer Relief Request)
*   **Why:** CRA collections officers cannot legally cancel your base tax debt, but they **can** cancel 100% of interest and penalties due to "financial hardship" or "extraordinary circumstances."
*   **Hardship Proof:** Your current year income is only $22,000 via T5 dividends. In British Columbia, this is well below the poverty line for an individual with family obligations, making payment of penalties mathematically impossible without depriving your household of basic shelter and food.

### Step B: Submit Form RC376 (Financial Disclosure)
*   **Why:** To get interest and penalties waived under financial hardship, you must prove you cannot afford it.
*   **Requirements:** Submit 3 months of personal bank statements, credit card statements, and your monthly shelter expenses (rent/mortgage, utilities). This forces the CRA to establish an interest-free, penalty-free monthly payment plan (e.g., $50/month) rather than attempting bank account freezing or wage garnishments.

### Step C: Request Administrative Freeze
*   Once the dispute letter and the RC4288 are filed, call CRA Collections and request an **administrative hold** on collections. Inform them that the debt is formally in dispute and under Taxpayer Relief review, which normally freezes aggressive enforcement actions.
